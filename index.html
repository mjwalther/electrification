The United States is currently experiencing the worst opioid epidemic in its history. The surge in misuse of opioids and opioid overdose deaths has surpassed the likelyhood of death resulting from car crashes or suicide.

In the 1990s and 2000s, the increase in opioid mortality was primarily related to prescription opioids. During this period, pharmaceutical companies downplayed the potential addictive effects of opioids.

However, after 2010, another spike of opioid overdose was largely driven by illegal opioids, such as heroin and synthetic fentanyl.

Although Purdue Pharma reformulated its OxyContin to prevent misuse in 2010, this intervention inadvertently worsened the opioid crisis. Many individuals turned to more dangerous and illicit street opioids.

Find out more about the current state of opioid prescriptions and fatalities below. You can also explore your county’s overdose rate and opioid prescription statistics from the recent 2022 data in our Mapping the Crisis section.

Opioid prescriptions have been decreasing.

In 2012, for every 1,000 residents, there were about 600 opioid prescriptions being given.

Ten years later, that rate of prescriptions has halved to around 300.

Yet, opioid overdoses have been on the rise.
